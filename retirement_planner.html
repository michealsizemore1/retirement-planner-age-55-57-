This is a simplified model: taxes use flat rates rather than IRS brackets (brokerage capital-gains tax is applied to the whole withdrawal, not just the gain portion, so real taxes are likely lower), and it doesn't model Medicare or one-time expenses. The plan runs through age 100 for both of you — specifically, through whichever spouse's life expectancy is later once translated to your age, so household spending doesn't stop early just because it's keyed to your age. Spouse's long-term care is modeled starting at her age 85 for 3 years, paid from Roth TSP first (tax-free, outside the normal order) and falling back to Brokerage then Traditional TSP if Roth runs short; your own long-term care is assumed to be covered by insurance and isn't charged against the portfolio. Withdrawals in retirement otherwise draw from brokerage first, then Traditional TSP, then Roth TSP last; checking/savings/HYSA are tracked as liquidity and not spent down. Rent, debt payments, and expenses are assumed covered by income before retirement and only enter the withdrawal need afterward. The spousal Social Security benefit assumes your spouse claims at the age you specify and that you've already filed for your own benefit. Four separate rates now drive different parts of the plan (all on the Assumptions page): General Inflation for most expenses and rent, Medical Inflation for Healthcare spending specifically, Social Security COLA for your SS and spousal benefit, and Housing Appreciation for home value — which only comes into play if you model a future purchase. The Monte Carlo score randomizes annual investment returns (not rent, debt, or guaranteed income) around your assumed averages — it's a directional signal, not a guarantee. The Transactions section is a standalone ledger for logging actual activity; it does not feed the projection, which runs on your Expenses assumptions instead. Treat all of this as a first pass — let me know what you'd like refined next.

Guaranteed Income

Social Security, spousal benefit, military pension, and VA disability. SS, spousal benefit, pension, and VA disability all grow using Social Security COLA (set on the Assumptions page) — pension and VA disability compensation are both COLA-adjusted in real life the same way Social Security is.

Guaranteed Income

Both the pension and VA disability are treated as already active today. Note: this simplified model doesn't apply income tax to any guaranteed income source, so the VA disability's tax-free status doesn't change the math yet — it's broken out separately for clarity and in case that changes later.
