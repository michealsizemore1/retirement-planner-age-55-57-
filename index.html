This is a simplified model, not tax or legal advice. Traditional TSP withdrawals are taxed using real 2026 federal marginal brackets (Married Filing Jointly) plus Idaho's flat state rate, based on the standard deduction and Social Security's 85%-taxable assumption on the Taxes page; brokerage withdrawals use federal long-term capital gains brackets plus Idaho's flat rate on the whole withdrawal (not just the gain, so real taxes are likely somewhat lower). Rule of 55 is assumed by default, so Traditional TSP withdrawals avoid the 10% early-withdrawal penalty before 59½ — toggle that off on the Taxes page to see the alternative. The plan runs through age 100 for both of you — specifically, through whichever spouse's life expectancy is later once translated to your age, so household spending doesn't stop early just because it's keyed to your age. Spouse's long-term care is modeled starting at her age 85 for 3 years, paid from Roth TSP first (tax-free, outside the normal order) and falling back to Brokerage then Traditional TSP if Roth runs short; your own long-term care is assumed to be covered by insurance and isn't charged against the portfolio. Withdrawals in retirement draw from brokerage first, then Traditional TSP, then Roth TSP last; checking/savings/HYSA are tracked as liquidity and not spent down. Medicare-aware healthcare premiums (Medical page) and any future/one-time expenses (Expenses page) are added on top of your recurring monthly expenses once you're retired. Rent, debt payments, and expenses are assumed covered by income before retirement and only enter the withdrawal need afterward — future/one-time expenses and windfalls are the exception, since those can hit at any age. The spousal Social Security benefit assumes your spouse claims at the age you specify and that you've already filed for your own benefit. Five separate rates now drive different parts of the plan (all on the Assumptions page): General Inflation for most expenses, rent, and future expenses, Medical Inflation for Healthcare spending and Medical page premiums, Social Security COLA for your SS, spousal benefit, and any COLA-adjusted annuity, and Housing Appreciation for home value — which only comes into play if you model a future purchase. The Monte Carlo score randomizes annual investment returns (not rent, debt, or guaranteed income) around your assumed averages — it's a directional signal, not a guarantee. The Transactions section is a standalone ledger for logging actual activity; it does not feed the projection, which runs on your Expenses assumptions instead. The Estate Planning & Insurance page is informational and doesn't feed the projection math. Treat all of this as a first pass — let me know what you'd like refined next.

Excess Income SavingsAutomatically invest a share of whatever's left over each year, on top of the contributions above

"Leftover" income = your job income, minus TSP/Roth/agency-match/brokerage contributions above, minus recurring expenses, housing, and debt payments for that year. Only applies while you're still working, and goes entirely into the single pie you pick above (edit pie names on the Investments page).

Without this, a paid-off debt (e.g. a car loan) just quietly lowers how much you're spending each year — the freed-up payment is still counted (your projection is correct either way), but it isn't visibly redirected anywhere. With this on, from the month a debt is paid off through retirement, its exact former monthly payment gets swept into the pie above every year on top of the % setting — so it shows up as real, trackable savings instead of just an invisible drop in spending. Only applies before retirement, while you're still earning a paycheck to redirect from — once retired, a paid-off debt still lowers how much your portfolio has to cover each year, it just no longer makes sense to withdraw money you don't need only to immediately reinvest it, which would trigger real, unnecessary tax.

Bracket & Tier Changes

Federal2026 brackets, Married Filing Jointly (10/12/22/24/32/35/37%)

Social Security and spousal benefit are treated as 85% taxable (typical once other income is substantial); VA disability stays fully tax-free and isn't included. Both pensions and annuity income are fully taxable.

Idaho State TaxFlat rate, applies to all taxable ordinary income and capital gains

FICA (Payroll Tax)Social Security + Medicare tax withheld from job income, working years only

Social Security tax is 6.2% of job income up to the wage base above (grown here with General Inflation as a stand-in for real wage-base growth). None of this applies to pension, Social Security, or investment withdrawal income — FICA only applies to job wages.

Rule of 55

Coach AI ChatOptional — only used if you add a key

This file has no backend — adding a key here lets the Ask AI panel (and the Coach page) call the Anthropic API directly from your browser, using your own account and usage. The key is stored only in this browser's local storage; it is never sent anywhere except directly to Anthropic when you ask a question. Each question sends a full snapshot of your plan — balances, income, expenses, debt, assumptions, withdrawal strategy, and the year-by-year projection — to Anthropic's servers, so it can give real, specific analysis rather than generic advice. The conversation itself is also saved locally in this browser (not sent anywhere beyond Anthropic) so it's still there next time you open the panel; use "New chat" in the panel to clear it. Leave the key blank to keep the AI fully offline.
